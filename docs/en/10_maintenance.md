# After the Program — How to Keep Living

By this point, several weeks have probably gone by. Some things went 
better, some worse, there were setbacks and there were days worth 
being proud of. This is the final part — about holding onto the 
result long-term, instead of just gritting your teeth for a month and 
sliding back to where you started.

## Progress Isn't Linear, and That's Permanent

Even six months in, there will be days when you sink into the feed 
longer than you'd like — stress, fatigue, a bad mood will always, from 
time to time, bring old patterns back. The difference isn't that 
relapses will never happen again — it's that you now have a tool to 
notice it faster and get back to balance faster, without a multi-day 
slide.

## Periodic Check-ins

Once a month or two, it's worth going back to simple tracking for a 
day or two — not because something necessarily went wrong, but as a 
preventive checkup. Barriers weaken over time: the app quietly ends up 
back on the home screen "just for convenience," notifications get 
switched back on unnoticed after an update. A short check-in every so 
often helps catch old habits quietly creeping back before they take 
hold again.

## Circumstances Change — So Should the Barriers

What worked against boredom at the office might not work against 
boredom on vacation. A new job, a move, a change in daily routine — 
any major life shift is worth treating as a cue to take a fresh look 
at your current triggers and barriers, rather than assuming a system 
set up once will keep working forever without revisiting it.

## Helping Others as Part of Maintenance

Oddly enough, one of the most durable ways to lock in your own 
progress is telling someone else about the path you went through — 
someone who recognizes themselves in the description. Explaining the 
mechanics of the addiction to another person reinforces your own 
understanding of what's happening to you, and that acts as extra 
protection against relapse.

## One Last Thing

You don't have to become someone who never watches short video at all. 
The goal of this methodology was never total abstinence — it's making 
the choice to watch or not watch yours again, consciously, instead of 
an automatic reaction programmed by an algorithm that has never seen 
you, but knows exactly how to hold your attention.

If one day you notice you're opening the feed because you genuinely 
want to, watching exactly as long as you planned, and closing it 
without guilt — that's the result all of this was for.
