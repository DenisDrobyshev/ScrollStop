# Questions People Actually Ask

## Isn't this just "use more willpower"?

No — it's closer to the opposite. Willpower is a resource that runs low
as the day goes on, which is exactly why "just resist it" fails by
evening. Most of this method is about changing your surroundings so the
choice doesn't come up as often, and about swapping the habit for
something that meets the same need. You spend less willpower, not more.

## I don't think I'm addicted. Is this still for me?

Probably, yes. "Addiction" is a heavy word and you don't need to earn it
to benefit here. If you sometimes lose more time than you meant to, or
find longer reads harder than they used to be, the same steps still
help — you'll just move through them faster. Read the
[Symptoms](2_symptoms.md) chapter and be honest with yourself, without
turning it into a diagnosis.

## Do I have to delete the apps?

No. For a lot of people, deleting everything on day one backfires and
leads to a bigger relapse a few days later. The method uses *friction*
instead of *bans*: turn off notifications, get the app off your home
screen, log out so it needs a password. You're making the feed slightly
inconvenient, not forbidden. The exact steps are in
[Week 2](5_friction.md).

## What about YouTube, Netflix, gaming, or doomscrolling the news?

The specifics here are aimed at short-form video, because its
few-seconds-per-clip design is uniquely good at hijacking attention. But
the underlying moves — watch your patterns, add friction, replace the
habit, plan for relapses — transfer cleanly to any compulsive digital
loop. If your problem is a different app, use the same structure and
adjust the details.

## How long until my attention comes back?

Longer than it took to lose it, and not on a straight line. People
usually notice the automatic reaching-for-the-phone easing within a
couple of weeks, while the deeper stuff — sitting with a long book,
holding focus for an hour — comes back over months. The brain is
plastic, so it does come back; it just doesn't happen on a schedule.
There's a whole chapter on it: [Rebuilding Focus](8_rebuilding_focus.md).

## I made it two weeks, then lost a whole evening to the feed. Did I blow it?

No. One bad evening doesn't erase two weeks of change, the same way one
bad meal doesn't erase a month of eating well. The thing that actually
does damage is the "I've already ruined it, so who cares" spiral that
turns one hour into a lost week. Notice the slip, name what triggered
it, and go do the next ordinary thing. Full chapter:
[On Relapses](7_relapses.md).

## Why isn't there an app that just locks me out?

Because an app that fights you is an app you learn to fight back — you
find the workaround, disable it on a bad day, and end up where you
started. The point of this method is to shift the habit itself, not to
build a cage around it. That said, there's a small, private tracker on
the [Week 1](4_awareness.md) page for counting your own opens; it lives
in your browser and sends nothing anywhere.

## Can I use this to get someone else off their phone?

You can share it, but you can't run it for them. Change like this only
sticks when the person actually wants it — policing someone else's
screen time usually just adds conflict. Send them the intro, and let
them decide. If it's your kid, the same holds, plus your own example
carries more weight than any rule you set.

## Is any of this a substitute for professional help?

No. If short-form video is seriously hurting your work, your
relationships, or your daily functioning, treat this as something you do
*alongside* talking to a professional, not instead of it.
