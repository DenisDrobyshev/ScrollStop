# The Plan on One Page

This is the whole method stripped down to what you actually do, day by
day. If you want the reasoning behind a step, each section links to the
full chapter — read those at least once, because the "why" is what
carries you through the days when motivation is gone. But if you just
want to start, everything you need is right here.

Two ground rules before the steps:

- Pick a start date in the next two days. Not "Monday", not "after the
  holidays" — this week, while you still care enough to have opened
  this page.
- Move one week at a time. Don't try to do all three weeks at once. The
  order matters more than the speed.

## Day zero — setup (about fifteen minutes)

- Decide how you'll count opens. The simplest thing that works: a note
  on your phone where you add one tally mark every time you open a
  short-video app. Nothing fancier.
- Write down in one plain sentence why you're doing this, and keep it
  where you'll stumble on it. Not as a motivational poster — you'll
  actually want to reread it around day nine.
- Change nothing else yet. No deleting apps, no blockers. Week one is
  measurement, and you can't measure a habit you've already disrupted.

## Week 1 — Watch yourself, change nothing

The goal is to find out what's really happening, not what you assume is
happening. ([Full chapter](4_awareness.md).)

Each day:

- Add a tally every time you open a short-video app. No judgment, just
  the count.
- A few times a day, when you catch yourself opening it, note one word
  for what set it off: bored, tired, anxious, waiting, avoiding
  something, or just habit.
- In the evening, write down the rough total time. Your phone's
  screen-time screen already tracks it.

At the end of the week, look at the numbers and circle the two triggers
that come up most. Those two are your targets for week two.

## Week 2 — Make it harder, one trigger at a time

The point is to spend less willpower by changing your surroundings
instead of gritting your teeth. Willpower runs out by evening;
environment doesn't. ([Full chapter](5_friction.md).)

Do these in order — one a day is fine if you want it gentle:

- Turn off every notification the app can send. All of them.
- Move it off your home screen into a folder on the last page, and log
  out so opening it costs a password.
- Put a small delay in your own way: make yourself search for the app
  or type the address instead of tapping a saved icon. Friction beats
  an outright block, because a block turns into a fight you eventually
  lose, while a few seconds of delay just gives the urge time to pass.
- For your top trigger from week one, decide the replacement in advance
  (see week three). For example: "when I reach for it while waiting in
  line, I open my book app instead."
- Keep the phone out of arm's reach in the one situation where you
  scroll most — usually the couch in the evening, or bed.

You're not banning anything this week. You're removing the automatic,
zero-effort path to the feed.

## Week 3 — Fill the gap, which is the part people skip

Remove the feed and put nothing in its place, and the emptiness will
push you straight back. The need behind the scrolling doesn't disappear
when the app does — it just goes looking for another outlet.
([Full chapter](6_replacement.md).)

- Make a short list of five things you can do instead, sorted by how
  much effort they take. Include at least two that take almost none: a
  specific playlist, a walk around the block, texting one person, ten
  push-ups, an article you saved.
- Write three "if-then" lines: "If I feel the pull *in this specific
  situation*, then I *do this specific thing*." Keep them concrete —
  vague plans are the ones that fail.
- When the urge hits, do the replacement for two minutes before you
  decide anything. Most of the time the urge is gone by then. And if it
  isn't and you open the app anyway — that's information, not failure.

## When you slip — and you will

One bad hour doesn't cancel two good weeks. The real trap isn't the
slip, it's the "well, I've already ruined it" spiral that turns one
hour into a lost evening. ([Full chapter](7_relapses.md).)

1. Notice it plainly: "I spent an hour in the app." No "again", no "I'm
   hopeless" — just the fact.
2. Name the trigger that set it off. That's the part you can actually
   use next time.
3. Go do the next ordinary thing in your day. Don't wait for a clean
   Monday to "start over" — there's nothing to start over.

## The evening rule

Most relapses happen late, when your self-control is already spent for
the day. One habit does most of the work here: the phone charges
outside the bedroom — or at least across the room — from about an hour
before sleep. If you use it as an alarm, a cheap alarm clock solves
that. ([Full chapter](9_evenings_and_sleep.md).)

## After the three weeks

- Once a week, glance at your screen-time number. That's your
  early-warning system, and it takes ten seconds.
- Expect bad days for good. They aren't relapses, they're weather. You
  only act when the trend climbs for a week or two, not on a single
  rough day.
- When the old convenience creeps back — the icon returns to the home
  screen, notifications sneak back on — just re-add the friction. This
  was never a one-time fix; it's a setting you readjust now and then.
  ([Full chapter](10_maintenance.md).)

## The entire thing in five lines

- Week 1: count, don't change anything.
- Week 2: kill notifications, hide the app, add a few seconds of
  friction.
- Week 3: decide your replacements in advance and use them for two
  minutes.
- Treat slips as data; react to trends, not to single days.
- The phone sleeps outside the bedroom.
