    Evenings and Sleep — A Separate Risk Zone¶

    Evening time, and especially bed before sleep, is probably the most common and most stubborn trigger of all. It deserves its own attention, because the mechanics here are a bit different than during the day.

    Why the Pull Is Stronger in the Evening¶

    By evening, your reserves of willpower and self-control are physically depleted — after a full day of decisions, tasks, and small strains, the brain is less able to resist automatic actions. On top of that, fatigue itself gets read as a reason for a bit of light entertainment, "I've earned a break." And then there's the bed itself — a place physically associated with relaxation, which further lowers already-depleted self-control.

    A Simple But Effective Rule¶

    The single most effective measure is physically not bringing your phone into bed. Not "face-down next to the pillow" — actually out of the bedroom, or at least the far corner of the room, far enough that you have to get up. If you need an alarm, buy a cheap separate alarm clock — that removes the last excuse to keep the phone by the bed.

    What to Do About "I Can't Fall Asleep"¶

    If you're lying down and can't sleep, the pull toward the phone isn't about boredom — it's a familiar falling-asleep ritual. Replace the ritual instead of trying to just tough out the emptiness. An audiobook or podcast at low volume, a few minutes of a simple breathing exercise, reading a physical book under dim light — any of these can gradually become the new ritual instead of scrolling.

    The Role of Screen Light¶

    It's also worth mentioning that bright screen light before bed disrupts the body's natural melatonin production, the hormone responsible for sleep onset — a separate, purely physiological mechanism from the psychological one. Even if the content in the feed were completely neutral, the simple act of staring at a bright screen late at night makes it harder for your body to prepare for sleep.

    Morning — The Second Vulnerable Point¶

    Symmetrically to the evening, morning right after waking up is also a common moment for automatically opening the feed, before conscious thought has even kicked in. The same physical-barrier logic applies here: if the phone isn't within arm's reach of the bed, and you have to get up and walk to it, the brain gets a second to switch on and make a conscious choice instead of a reflexive one.
